Babylonian Approximation of Square Roots

An ancient technique for estimating square roots

The Babylonians, over 3,000 years ago, developed an iterative method to approximate the square root of numbers, particularly the square root of 2. This method, often called the "Babylonian method" or "Heron's method," is a precursor to modern numerical techniques.

Mathematical Foundation

We begin with an initial guess \( x_0 \) and refine it using the formula:

\[ x_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2} \left( x_n + \frac{S}{x_n} \right) \]

where:

  • \( x_n \) is the current approximation of \( \sqrt{S} \).
  • \( S \) is the number whose square root we seek.

  • Second Step: \( x_2 = \frac{1}{2} \left(1.5 + \frac{2}{1.5} \right) = \frac{1}{2} (1.5 + 1.3333) \approx 1.4167 \)
  • Third Step: \( x_3 = \frac{1}{2} \left(1.4167 + \frac{2}{1.4167} \right) \approx 1.4142 \)

As we see, the approximation rapidly converges to \( \sqrt{2} \approx 1.4142 \), demonstrating the effectiveness of this method.

Applications and Legacy

The Babylonian method is still used in modern numerical computing due to its rapid convergence. It highlights the ingenuity of early mathematicians and their understanding of iterative approximation.
